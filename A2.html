Article 2

 

Hundreds of years ago, it was fairly widely known among rural peoples and herbalists that a broth made from shavings of willow bark boiled in water would help to reduce a fever or ameliorate the pain of a headache, a toothache, or an inflamed joint. Today we know that this primitive folk medicine was the forerunner of the miracle drug aspirin. It was first marketed just prior to the end of the nineteenth century and has long-since become a staple in almost every medicine cabinet in the civilized world. Few among us can say that they haven’t at some time used aspirin to treat a headache, a fever, arthritis, or muscle pain. Without aspirin, modern man would be in a sorry state indeed.

 

 

In recent years many people have begun to shy away from aspirin (acetylsalicylic acid) in favor of analgesics containing acetaminophen or ibuprofen because of its ability to irritate the lining of the stomach and cause bleeding, or because of the danger of fatality from Reye's syndrome in children. But others are turning to aspirin as a panacea because it has been postulated that a daily dosage of aspirin will help to mitigate against heart attacks and strokes by inhibiting the body’s production of prostaglandin which can cause clotting.

 


Some people have imputed other beneficial affects to aspirin which are as yet unproven and may be of questionable veracity. For example, it has been claimed that aspirin will prevent or even cure certain types of cancer, will help to delay the onset of Alzheimer’s disease, or will greatly reduce the effects of certain neurological dysfunctions such as Parkinson’s disease. Such claims should be taken with a grain of salt.
